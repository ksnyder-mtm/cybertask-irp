What is Incident Response Planning?

A structured approach to handling security breaches or cyber attacks

Think of it as your business's "fire drill" for cyber attacks:

  • Preparation: Having a plan before trouble strikes
  • Detection: Knowing when something's wrong
  • Response: Taking immediate action to limit damage
  • Recovery: Getting back to business safely
  • Learning: Improving for next time

Why Are Small Businesses Targeted?

43%
of cyber attacks target small businesses
60%
of small companies go out of business within 6 months of a cyber attack

Why attackers choose small businesses:

  • Easier targets: Often lack dedicated IT security
  • Valuable data: Customer info, financial records, business secrets
  • Weaker defenses: Limited security budgets and tools
  • Quick payoffs: More likely to pay ransoms to get back to business

The Real Cost of Being Unprepared

Direct Financial Impact

  • Average cost: $200,000 per incident
  • Ransom payments: $10,000 - $50,000+
  • System restoration costs

Business Disruption

  • Average downtime: 23 days
  • Lost productivity and revenue
  • Customer service interruptions

Long-term Damage

  • Customer trust and reputation
  • Legal liability and compliance issues
  • Competitive disadvantage

Bottom-Line Security Essentials

These four basics will prevent 90% of attacks

Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

Require a second form of identification beyond just passwords

Start with email, banking, and business apps

Password Managers

Use unique, strong passwords for every account

Popular options: Bitwarden, 1Password, Dashlane

Regular Backups

Follow the 3-2-1 rule: 3 copies, 2 different media, 1 offsite

Test your backups regularly!

Cyber Awareness Training

Train staff to recognize phishing and social engineering

Regular, short training sessions work best

Why MFA is crucial:

  • Blocks 99.9% of automated attacks
  • Protects even if passwords are stolen
  • Easy to set up on most business apps

Quick Wins - Enable MFA on:

  • Business email (Gmail, Outlook, etc.)
  • Banking and financial accounts
  • Cloud storage (Dropbox, OneDrive, etc.)
  • Social media business accounts

Cyber Liability Insurance: Your Safety Net

What is Cyber Liability Insurance?

Insurance that helps cover costs related to cyber incidents and data breaches

What it typically covers:

Incident Response

IT forensics, system restoration, legal counsel

Business Interruption

Lost income during downtime

Notification Costs

Customer notification, credit monitoring

Legal Protection

Regulatory fines, lawsuits, settlements

Key Considerations:

  • Coverage limits should match your risk exposure
  • Look for policies that include incident response services
  • Understand what security measures are required
  • Consider bundling with general liability insurance

Your Incident Response Team

Even small businesses need defined roles

Incident Commander

Usually: Business owner or manager

  • Makes key decisions
  • Coordinates response efforts
  • Communicates with stakeholders

Technical Lead

Could be: IT person, tech-savvy employee, or external consultant

  • Handles technical response
  • Works with IT vendors
  • Manages system recovery

Communications Lead

Usually: Manager or administrative staff

  • Handles customer communications
  • Manages media if needed
  • Coordinates with legal counsel

For very small businesses: One person may wear multiple hats, but still define the roles and have backup contacts

When an Incident Happens: First Steps

Immediate Actions (First Hour)

  1. Stay calm - Panic leads to poor decisions
  2. Contain the threat - Disconnect affected systems if safe to do so
  3. Document everything - Take photos, screenshots, notes
  4. Activate your response team - Call your key contacts
  5. Notify your insurance - Many policies require quick notification

DON'T Do These Things:

  • Don't pay ransoms immediately
  • Don't turn off systems randomly
  • Don't try to "fix it yourself" without expertise
  • Don't communicate with attackers directly
  • Don't ignore legal notification requirements

Golden Rule: When in doubt, ask for help. It's better to over-respond than under-respond.

Testing Your Plan

A plan that's never tested is just wishful thinking

Tabletop Exercises

Gather your team and walk through scenarios

Frequency: Quarterly
  • What if our email system is down?
  • What if customer data is compromised?
  • What if our website is defaced?

Technical Tests

Test your backups and recovery procedures

Frequency: Monthly
  • Can you actually restore from backups?
  • Do your emergency contacts work?
  • Are your passwords and MFA working?

Regular Maintenance

  • Update contact information
  • Review and update procedures
  • Train new employees
  • Update insurance coverage as business grows
